Fun fact: this mod started out as a sandbox for another mod.

As funny as it sounds, it’s actually true. Right after making Little Big Man, I noted that mod would likely be my last standalone mod. This wasn’t entirely false, given I ended up working on other mods with bigger teams (Hope, Moonbeam, A Billionaire A Revolutionary) and wanted to do a more collaborative effort regarding one unfinished mod whose team I was a part of, before the mod collapsed on itself and I was left with the remains of it (Deandemocracy). But as you can see, it wasn’t entirely true either.

The initial plan was for me to do a mod called The Dreaming Moon, which I can best explain as a loose Bill Clinton second term simulator… through the eyes of Hillary. Bad timing, however, prevented me from actually developing this mod, which was one of the initial ideas I had for A Lifetime of This, back when it was still a story called The Morning Papers.

I joked about making a cynical Charlie Brown mod to see how it would develop, aware that there was a Reddit mockup about this (though it was set in the 70s, and there was no such cynicalness). I learned from this process that I can’t write anything cynical, but it was a good experience anyway. I wanted to see what happened when the gang grew up, became teenagers, and actually had real stakes applied to their rivalries, and I think I was able to develop it well enough in some corners.

Some may wonder why “The Major Leagues” — when you think of it, you may imagine some sort of elite group (or maybe just Major League Baseball), and in some way there are several of these in the mod. But what is Charlie Brown’s relationship to the “major leagues” here? This answer varies, depending on who you’re thinking of (Liuns, Patty, the school cliques, the administration). Charlie is not a part of the major leagues, and maybe he doesn’t want to be part of one. But here, the word “leagues” also signifies a greater distance. Between whom? Between him and Linus, and this distance is, aside from being phyiscal, emotional. And in some way, this election is how you navigate through it.

Either way, I do hope I’ve been able to live up to your expectations of this mod, as low as they might’ve been. What else would you expect from a Peanuts mod, honestly?

Well, at least they tried.

Questions & answers

A few questions answered about the world of Sparky Memorial High, this mod, and other stuff:

  • Why a student council election?
    Because the cutthroat, reputation-ruining politics of a high school election perfectly mirror the existential dread Charlie Brown feels in his life here. Setting it in high school allowed the characters to be, heh, “cynical” enough to actually hurt each other while remaining fundamentally immature. Not only that, but, technically-speaking, the idea of having two separate elections seemed much more interesting than making up a separate map for this, which wouldn’t be as cool. Charlie could visit as many groups as he wanted, but if he made little to no overtures to them, it would’ve been mostly pointless.
  • Why are Charlie Brown and Linus fighting? They used to be best friends.
    They were, and that’s the tragedy of it. You see, as they’ve gotten older, they’ve sort of drifted apart. Linus became more ambitious and image-conscious, aligning himself with the school establishment (the teachers, the administration, the honors students, et al.) to build what he sees as his path to a successful future. Charlie, feeling left behind and seeing his friend as a sellout, became more withdrawn. In the process, Linus’s overtures to Charlie over time have been met in different ways, but often with silence. The student council election isn’t the cause of their falling out, but to both, it seems like the public stage for a friendship that has been quietly breaking down for years now.
  • Why are you using the 2000 election as a backdrop?
    It’s a stylistic and thematic choice, but also kind of an obvious one in retrospect. 2000 is a fascinating year to set anything in, because it sits right at the edge of a cliff that nobody knew was there yet. The 90s were supposed to be America’s victory lap: the Cold War was over, the economy was booming, and there was this general sense that things were, broadly, fine. And then, one by one, the cracks started showing: Lewinsky, Columbine, Kosovo, the whole circus of Bush v. Gore, and eventually 9/11, which didn’t just end the decade but retroactively poisoned the memory of it. The Clintonian glory days, it appeared, turned out to be a little less glorious than advertised.

    And so The Major Leagues uses the 2000 election’s dates, candidate structure, and sense of a close, nation-dividing contest to give the high school drama a feeling of inflated (or maybe absurd!) importance. To these teenagers, their school election feels as significant as a real presidential race, if not more. And so, to an extent, it maps the themes of establishment-vs-outsider and a fractured electorate onto the personal conflict between Charlie and Linus — two kids who, like the decade itself, used to get along a lot better than they do now.
  • What’s Lucy’s deal? Is she helping or hurting?
    Both. Lucy’s primary motivation is chaos and maintaining her own power as the school’s social puppet master. She despises her brother’s self-righteousness and wants to see him humbled, which is why she offers Charlie dirt. But Lucy is a more complex character than she looks like, however. Here, she also has a complicated, almost fond attachment to Charlie, and not only because he is a perennial underdog. Leaking the depression rumors is her way of testing Charlie and stirring the pot. Ultimately, her actions aren’t about helping Charlie win; they’re about making the game more interesting for herself. And it is not something that makes her happy.
  • What’s the deal with Schroeder? He seems so pretentious.
    He is… to an extent. Think of Schroeder as someone who has retreated into his art as a defense mechanism against what he sees as the crushing banality of high school. He’s built an ivory tower for himself in the music room. And his endorsement isn’t about who the better person is; it’s just a decision about which candidate is less likely to disrupt his world and more likely to protect the arts programs. Linus, with his establishment ties, seems like the safer, more “civilized” choice. Winning Schroeder’s support requires you to speak his language, whether that’s through calculated promises (funding for the arts), emotional appeals (reminding him of the Van Pelts’ past transgressions), or simply by proving you’re not the philistine he assumes you are.
  • What do Marcie and Franklin represent in the story?
    They represent two different forms of the establishment that Charlie has to contend with. Marcie is the press: objective, analytical, and occasionally neutral. She’s not his enemy, but she’s not his ally either; her job is to report the truth as she sees it, and for most of the campaign, the truth is that Charlie Brown is a long-shot candidate. Winning her over means proving you’re not just a protest vote but a viable leader. Franklin, on the other hand, is the political establishment. He’s the moderate, the institutionalist who believes in the system and wants to reform it from within. And he’s a crucial test for Charlie: does he compromise his revolutionary message to win his endorsement and gain legitimacy, or does he stay pure and risk alienating his coalition?
  • Is Roy really the best campaign manager?
    No, not really, although he may not be the worst. Roy, while interested in hanging out with Charlie, is not at all concerned about the school election, and so he gives Charlie a lot of headaches as his campaign manager. Whether you think Roy enables Charlie’s worst instincts depends a lot on your interpretation of their relationship, though.
  • What’s the difference between having Patty and Roy as a campaign manager?
    A huge one, depending on your goals. Patty is a true believer, but she’s also a pragmatist who wants to win, even if it means getting her hands dirty. If she quits, it’s because she feels Charlie’s strategy is too idealistic or naive to succeed. Roy, on the other hand, is occasionally described as incompetent and apathetic. He’s only there because Patty was, as much as he likes and respects Charlie.
  • How important are the different school cliques?
    They are the closest thing you have to an “electoral map” here. Each of the five main factions — the Jocks, the Burnouts, the Honors Kids, the Arts/Theatre crowd, and the Administration — has its own set of values and priorities. The Jocks generally value strength and respect Patty; the Burnouts are anti-establishment and respond to populist anger; the Honors Kids value stability and favor the status quo candidate, Linus; the Arts kids are idealists who respond to emotion but are turned off by overt aggression; and the Administration wants a student president who won’t rock the boat. You can, technically, win them all, although this is quite difficult. The core strategy of the game however, is building a winning coalition from these disparate groups, which often have competing interests. Your choices will almost always please one group at the expense of another.
  • What exactly is the ‘Ruthlessness’ stat?
    It tracks Charlie’s descent into a Machiavellian political operator (or not, that is). If you go too far, you compromise your morals, you alienate your friends, and you become exactly the kind of establishment monster you swore you were running against. High ruthlessness alters your endings drastically.
  • What does the ‘Cynicism’ stat do?
    If ‘Ruthlessness’ is about your actions, ‘Cynicism’ is about your attitude. A highly cynical Charlie is sarcastic, nihilistic, and sees the entire political process as a joke; he’s more likely to be demotivated and give up. A low-cynicism (or idealistic?) Charlie believes that real change is possible and that the system can be fixed. This stat affects your tone throughout the gameplay, such as in debates, how characters like Patty and Franklin perceive you, and pushes you to different narrative paths and endings. A very cynical Charlie might find that even winning feels empty.
  • How does the “Linus Bond” stat actually work? Does keeping it high help me win?
    Not directly, no. The Linus Bond is a stat that tracks the state of your personal relationship. You can absolutely win the election while driving your bond with him into the negative — in fact, some of the most ruthless (and effective) strategies require you to do just that! Conversely, you can maintain a high bond and still lose the election, and vice-versa. What it does affect is the narrative, the tone of your interactions, and the ultimate outcome of the story. A high bond unlocks more poignant, reflective, and even reconciliatory endings, where victory or defeat is secondary to the fate of your friendship, while a low bond leads to bitter, confrontational, and lonely conclusions. Ultimately, are you running to win, or are you running to save what’s left of your friend?
  • What does Linus’s “Great Pumpkin of our Potential” speech actually mean?
    In his metaphor, the “Great Pumpkin” is the school administration and the established system of power. The student body are the children waiting in the pumpkin patch, and “sincerity” is his term for playing by the rules: being a model student, showing deference, and not causing trouble. The “reward” is the administration’s favor: better funding, more privileges, and a seat at the table. That’s it. Linus, always a coach at heart, believes that working within the system is the only way to achieve anything. Charlie’s counter-argument is that this is a lie. He believes the game is rigged, the administration doesn’t care about sincerity, and waiting patiently in the patch is nothing more than a fool’s errand.
  • The endings are pretty intense. What’s the philosophy behind them?
    The goal was to create endings where winning or losing the election is only half the story. The real outcome is about who Charlie Brown becomes in the process. Did you win, but at the cost of your soul and your oldest friendship? Did you lose, but maintain your integrity and earn the respect of the people who matter? I like to think that the endings are designed to be character studies that reflect the cumulative weight of your choices. There isn’t a single “golden” path where everyone is happy and you win by a landslide, naturally. Every victory comes with a cost, and every loss offers its own strange, quiet kind of grace.
  • What’s the story with all the bizarre alternate-history ending slides?
    You tell me! Think of it as small choices having huge consequences. Just as your decisions as Charlie Brown determine the fate of the student council, the outcome of the national 2000 election on your screen dictates the entire political reality of the country in the years that follow. It’s also a way of breaking the fourth wall and reminding the player that this entire scenario is just a quirky little mod.
  • Where is Snoopy during all of this?
    He’s chilling at home, mostly, but he’s doing alright. He appears prominently on Election Night when the weight of the campaign finally breaks you, as perhaps the only pure thing left in Charlie’s life.
  • Where is Rerun during all of this?
    Dead, probably. Just kidding, he’s alive, but because the story mostly focuses on the election, it was hard to include much of him here. All you need to know is that he is, unsurprisingly, a supporter of Linus.
  • What happened with Heather (The Little Red-Haired Girl)?
    Charlie’s feelings for her are weaponized early in the campaign. If your notebook gets leaked by Linus’s camp, you can either take the high road or vow vengeance. Either way, her brief appearance in the hallway proves that high school can be a very cold place. What I can say is that their relationship isn’t ruined or anything after the campaign, though it does make the moment a bit awkward whenever it’s brought up.

Living in a World of Barack (or close to it)

by StrawberryMaster

Fun fact: this mod started out as a sandbox for another mod.

When working on 2016: A Lifetime of This, I ended up doing some snooping and was able to recover most of the original chunk of questions for the 2012 mod; for those unaware, the original 2012 Dan Bryan scenario had about ~80 or so questions, and in the early stages of the game you could have an option to answer those 80 questions if you wanted (or a smaller number, like 40 or 60 questions). I tested these out in the original 2012 mod and it worked fairly well, though I ended up making my own adjustments to it for easier loading. A few changes here and there, and I realized I got too carried away. But by then, I already had a lot of stuff that I just couldn’t slap on top of 2016ALOT, and I decided to work on what eventually became “Little Big Man”.

Honestly, working on this was very fun, especially when curating this mod’s soundtrack. Hopefully, you have enjoyed some of the music choices here (or the volume slider at 0%, which also works out well). I never thought I would make a mod so soon after 2016ALOT, and so I’m happy that I was able to dedicate myself at least more than a bit to this funny mod. I especially appreciate the warm welcome that I received from the communit during my stay here. You guys rock!

I do need to disclose that this is, probably, my last standalone scenario. It has been quite a ride, but it’s also been very stressful at times, particularly because most of this was the work of a one-person team. You might see me work with someone on a future mod, but this may be my last standalone mod for a while. Regardless, I do hope I’ve been able to live up to your expectations of this mod, as low as they might’ve been.

Recommended readings

  • John Sides and Lynn Vavreck - The Gamble: Choice and Chance in the 2012 Presidential Election (Updated Edition)

    An excellent analysis of the 2012 campaign. It argues that despite the daily drama of the campaign trail, the election's outcome was largely determined by political and economic fundamentals, like the state of the economy and presidential approval, and not by the so-called "game changers" mentioned throughout the campaign. Here is a snippet:

    As the 2012 campaign got under way, there were at least three bets being made. One was by Democrats, who were betting on an incumbent who, while arguably the favorite, was still facing challenging economic times and a pessimistic public. One was by Republicans, who were betting on which of their many candidates could do something rare: defeat a sitting incumbent president. And one was by voters, who were betting on which candidate would best help the country recover from the worst economic recession in over seventy years.

  • Dan Balz - Collision 2012: Obama vs. Romney and the Future of Elections in America

    Another quite useful read, this books talk about how the 2012 election changed the course of American politics, and also the impacts it had on America’s electoral process. Here is a snippet:

    It was not an uplifting campaign by any stretch of the imagination. At times the plain nuttiness of it all cried out for Hunter S. Thompson to chronicle it. But both as political theater and as a window into the struggle between where America has been and where it may be going, it was as engaging as it was instructive about the state of our politics.

  • John Heilemann and Mark Halperin - Double Down: Game Change 2012

    The sequel to the bestselling book on the 2008 election, this provides a behind-the-scenes account of the Obama and Romney campaigns. It details the strategic decisions, internal conflicts, and critical moments that shaped the race. Here is a snippet:

    OBAMA LAUGHED WHEN HE heard that Romney had described him as Santa Claus in chief, doling out presents to the freeloaders gathered around the White House Christmas tree. “He must have really meant that 47 percent thing,” the president remarked to his aides.

  • Michael Kranish and Scott Helman - The Real Romney

    An in-depth biography of Mitt Romney, tracing his life through his Mormon faith, his family, his successful career at Bain Capital, and his political evolution. It offers a fairly comprehensive portrait of the real life Republican nominee. Here is a snippet:

    Something else stood out to Mitt’s peers: his bond with his father, who remained his hero and confidant notwithstanding the friction over Mitt’s home visits. Classmates could see the closeness between them. “It was especially interesting,” Romney’s classmate Mike Roake said, “because we were freshmen and therefore in the process of divorcing ourselves from our parents.”

  • The Washington Post Staff - Obama's Last Stand: POLITICO Playbook 2012

    A collection of reporting from The Washington Post that provides a account of the campaign as it unfolded, focusing on the key challenges facing the Obama campaign. Though not as long as the others, it's one of the most fascinating. Here is a snippet:

    Obama had always expressed a gnawing fear of political defeat in sports terms, comparing the shame to that of a high school basketball player blowing last-second free throws in front of his friends and family. Getting beat was to have the word “loser” “flashing through people’s minds,” he wrote in the 2004 edition of Dreams from My Father.

  • Barack Obama - A Promised Land

    Although published after his presidency, the first volume of Obama's memoirs covers his first term in detail, including the legislative battles over the economic stimulus and the Affordable Care Act, which were central issues in the 2012 campaign. Not only that, but it's an essential book for anyone who wants to know more about Barack himself. Here is a snippet:

    Michelle bore the brunt of all this, shuttling between mothering and work, unconvinced that she was doing either job well. At the end of each night, after feeding and bath time and story time and cleaning up the apartment and trying to keep track of whether she’d picked up the dry cleaning and making a note to herself to schedule an appointment with the pediatrician, she would often fall into an empty bed, knowing the whole cycle would start all over again in a few short hours while her husband was off doing “important things.”

  • Mitt Romney - No Apology: The Case for American Greatness

    Today we find ourselves at a very different moment in history, and I fear that if we remain on our current track, history will come to know us as this nation’s worst generation—because we will force our children and their children to bear the brunt of our recklessness and the willful neglect of the problems we created.
